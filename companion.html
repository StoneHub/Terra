The laws of Terra

Four rules govern everything below — and the final one is real Ruby, not game fiction.

⚡ The echo is the UI

IRB prints every expression's return value. Terra draws itself through that echo: world is a map because World#inspect returns one. If you can see it, it's a real object you can hold.

⏳ Time waits for you

There is no engine loop. Nothing moves until you spend days explicitly: pass for one, pass 7 for a week. Between commands the world is a photograph.

❄️ Seasons are state

winter! and spring! reversibly mutate climate. lake.ice_over! and lake.thaw! do the same locally. These are game mechanics, not Ruby freezing.

🥶 The Great Freeze is forever

great_freeze! calls World#freeze. That override adds Terra's ending, then super deliberately invokes Ruby's real Object#freeze: shallow and irreversible. big_bang! binds a fresh World; nothing is unfrozen.

Getting started

Six steps from an empty void to an ecosystem with your fingerprints on it. Keep this page beside your terminal. (In a hurry? eden! conjures a ready-made world. Stuck? inspire deals a ritual from the grimoire below.)

1

Enter the void

From the Terra folder. This opens IRB — Ruby's interactive shell — with your powers pre-loaded. Everything you type is real Ruby, evaluated the moment you press return.

zsh
$ bin/terra
✨  T E R R A  ✨
You are a god. Type `powers` to see your abilities.
2

Let there be light

The world starts as void — nothing can exist until you illuminate it. :light is a symbol: Ruby's lightweight name-value, the idiom for options from a fixed set.

irb — terra
>> let_there_be :light
And there was light. 🌅

Make something — and keep your grip on it

Assign what you spawn to a variable. It isn't a menu selection; it's a live Ruby object you now hold. The keyword arguments (at:, size:, name:) are all optional — omit them and Terra improvises.

irb — terra
>> lake = spawn :lake, at: [3, 4], name: "Mirrormere"
 3  🟩🟩🟩🌊🟩🟩
 4  🟩🟩🌊🌊🌊🟩
 5  🟩🟩🟩🌊🟩🟩
=> 🌊 Lake "Mirrormere" — 5 tiles centered near (3, 4)
4

Change it after the fact

The object stays alive. Rename it with plain assignment, mutate it with its own methods — the map redraws itself after every act.

irb — terra
>> lake.ice_over!
 3  🟩🟩🟩🧊🟩🟩
 4  🟩🟩🧊🧊🧊🟩
 5  🟩🟩🟩🧊🟩🟩
=> 🧊 Lake "Mirrormere" — 5 tiles centered near (3, 4)
>> lake.thaw!
5

Breathe life, then spend some days

let_there_be :life unlocks creatures. Animals hold still until you pass — then rabbits wander, ferns spread, old growth dies. The day counter in the map header is your clock.

irb — terra
>> let_there_be :life
🌱 And the world drew breath…
>> spawn :rabbit, count: 3
⏳ Nothing will move until you `pass`.
>> pass 6
☀️  Terra — day 6
 7  🟩🟩🟩🐇🟩🌿🌿🟩
 8  🟩🐇🟩🟩🟩🌿🟩🟩

6

Write a creature's brain

The finale: hand spawn a block and it replaces the animal's instinct. Your block runs once per day, every day, forever. (Ruby quirk, learned honestly: a brace block needs the parens — spawn(:rabbit) { … } — or use do…end.)

irb — terra
>> pilgrim = spawn(:rabbit) { |r| r.hop_toward :water }
=> 🐇 Rabbit at (11, 0) — age 0, divine brain
>> pass 10
…and the pilgrim is at the lakeshore.

IRB survival kit

The shell is half the lesson. These work in any IRB, not just Terra.

↑ / ↓

Walk your command history. Re-run or edit your last act of creation.

_

The last returned value. Forgot to assign your mountain? mtn = _ rescues it.

Tab

Autocomplete — press the actual Tab key. It knows the class of variables (lake.i → ice_over!) but not of method results; for those, take a handle first: w = world, then w.fe.

Ctrl-C

Abandon the line you're typing. Ctrl-D or exit leaves IRB entirely.

The => echo

IRB prints every expression's return value. In Terra that echo is the UI — the map is just world.inspect.

Nothing persists

Exiting ends the universe. bin/terra starts a fresh void every time.

Life

Unlocked by let_there_be :life. Creatures act once per day, only when days pass.

Left alone, populations find their own equilibrium: spreading fills the land, lifespans clear it. pass 30 and watch. Or skip the placement decisions entirely: sow 12 scatters seeds and lets each terrain grow its native — sow 6, on: :sand to target one biome. Seeds landing on stone are lost, and the log says how many.

Weather — the sky has moods

The map header is the forecast. Skies shift on their own as days pass (sticky — most days hold), or bend to a word: let_there_be :rain / :snow / :storm / :clear. world.weather asks directly.

SkyEffect while it lasts
☀️ clearThe baseline. Nothing special — enjoy it.
🌧️ rainPlants spread at double chance. Green things love it.
❄️ snowPlants pause entirely (they still age). Animals don't mind.
⛈️ stormWild lightning may scorch a random tile each day — and it kills what stands there, remains and all. Nature's smite, aimed by no one.

Divine brains — blocks as behavior

A block handed to spawn replaces an animal's instinct entirely; Terra calls it once per day with the animal itself. This is the Ruby block in its natural role: behavior as an argument.

irb — terra
>> spawn(:rabbit) { |r| r.hop_toward :water }   # a pilgrim
>> spawn(:hawk)   { |h| h.hop_toward [0, 0] }   # a homing hawk
>> spawn :tortoise do |t|                        # do…end works bare
..   t.age.even? ? t.wander : t.stay
.. end

Verbs a brain can use: wander, hop_toward(:forest), hop_toward([x, y]), stay — plus anything else the animal answers: age, tile, nearest(:water). Plants ignore blocks; they have no will of their own.

Death, removal, and what fills the hole

EventWhat actually happens
Plant reaches its lifespanIt dies quietly and vanishes — no corpse, the tile simply shows terrain again. (Its offspring usually outlive it.)
Animal grows oldNothing. Animals are currently immortal — nothing hunts, nothing starves. Food chains are Level 3's problem.
smite at: [x, y]The tile scorches, anything standing on it dies by name in the log — and leaves 💀 remains that fade after ~2 days of passed time.
unmake lakeThe feature's tiles revert to plains — water never replaces land; water only exists where a lake does. Tiles another feature claimed later stay theirs.
unmake rabbit / unmake herdReturns to dust, single or plural. Removal is instant; no time needs to pass.

Targeting & smiting

Coords are for places, references are for things. The difference is a Ruby lesson wearing a thundercloud.

Find things on the map

irb — terra
>> world.animals
=> [🐇 Rabbit at (1, 1) — age 3, instinct, 🐺 Wolf at (5, 1) — age 2, instinct]
>> world.rabbits          # the plural of ANY species is a method — even world.lilies
>> world.at(5, 1)
=> 🟩 (5, 1) plains · standing here: 🐺 Wolf at (5, 1) — age 2, instinct

Every expression echoes through inspect, and Terra's creatures print with emoji and coords — the REPL is your radar. (Writing generous inspect methods is a habit that makes real-world debugging bearable too.)

Lost the reference?

Spawned something without assigning it? Nothing is lost — the world holds every reference, and you can fish yours back out:

irb — terra
>> world.features                                      # everything, named, with coords
>> woods = world.features.find { |f| f.name == "Mirkwood" }
>> woods = _                                            # or: _ is the last result

unmake and smite also accept a kind (:forest) or a feature's name ("Mirkwood") directly — but only when exactly one thing matches. Two lakes and unmake :lake will list both and make you choose. Names are ambiguous; references never are.

Two ways to aim

InvocationWhat it hits
smite at: [5, 1] or smite 5, 1The place. Splash damage to whatever stands there — and it can miss, if the wolf moved since you looked.
smite wolfThe thing. A live object reference tracks its target anywhere. Never misses.
smite herdArrays work too — one bolt per member.

Coming from Kotlin: this is the pointer/value intuition made visceral. A coordinate is a value describing where something was; a variable holds a reference to what it is.

Targeting queries — a taste of Level 3

The "targeting system" is just Enumerable over live objects. Providence will build on exactly this:

irb — terra
>> wolf = world.animals.find { |a| a.kind == :wolf }   # first match
>> world.animals.max_by(&:age)                          # the elder
>> world.animals.select { |a| a.near?(lake) }           # spatial query (range: 2 default)
>> world.rabbits.each { |r| smite r }                    # the flood, basically

Creator's workshop

The stock world is a starter kit. These are the tools for filling it out.

A bigger canvas

Worlds are born with their size — there is no stretching a live one. Ask the next universe for more room (up to 40×30; past ~25 wide the map may wrap in a narrow terminal):

irb — terra
>> big_bang! width: 20, height: 12
The old universe collapses behind you. A new void awaits. ✨

Invent a species

ordain writes a new entry in the book of species — then spawn it like any native. Under the hood it's one line: KINDS[:wolf] = { … } in an ordinary, deliberately-unfrozen Hash. No new class required; a species is just data. Ordained species last for the session — when one earns permanence, copy its line into lib/terra/animal.rb or plant.rb.

irb — terra
>> ordain :wolf, emoji: "🐺", habitat: :land, speed: 3
>> spawn :wolf, count: 2
>> ordain :cactus, emoji: "🌵", grows_on: [:sand], spread: 0.05, lifespan: 40
>> spawn :cactus
>> spawn(:wolf) { |w| w.hop_toward :forest }   # brains work on your species too

Animal knobs: habitat: (:land / :water / :air) and speed: (tiles per day). Plant knobs: grows_on: (terrain list), spread: (0–1 chance per day), lifespan: (days).

Write the scripture

Every act — genesis, spawns, smitings, the passing of days, even the Great Freeze — is recorded with a map snapshot. chronicle prints the recent past; chronicle! renders the whole history to terra-chronicle.html, an illuminated page you can open in a browser or keep as a memorial to an ended world. Under the hood it's ERB — the same stdlib templating Rails views are made of; read lib/terra/chronicle.html.erb to see Ruby woven into markup.

irb — terra
>> chronicle
Day   0 — 🌊 Lake "Mirrormere" forms — 5 tiles near (5, 1)
Day   5 — ⏳ 5 days pass (+14 souls)
Day   5 — ⚡ Lightning strikes (3, 3) — 🌿 The smite claims the fern.
>> chronicle!
